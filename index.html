Artist Statement

On kinesthesis, robotic presence, and the poetry of machine gesture.

My research sits at the intersection of art, robotics, and machine learning. I approach machines not as automation tools but as collaborators in a philosophical investigation of kinesthesis — how movement is sensed, generated, and made meaningful from within a body that inhabits its own operative world. In my practice, I create robotic sculptures that react and gesture. I build autonomous figures that develop their own forms of behavior, challenging how we understand motion, sensitivity, and presence in robotic art. Rather than reproducing life, I ask how robotic systems can embody meaning through gesture and articulation.

Robotic movement and presence provoke human response through unfamiliar behaviors. I am interested in uncanny motion — gestures that emerge from feedback and timing yet feel estranged from habitual rhythms. Such motions do not mimic the human; they generate mechanical expressivity from internal logics, learned behavior, and a kind of awareness — the machine's registration of its own position, force, and trajectory in space. Robotic design can extend beyond task-based function to become a form of poetic inquiry. Hardware and programming are not tools; they are conceptual sites where motion is generated, constrained, and made meaningful. These technical limits shape gestures and guide machine behavior.

Using reinforcement learning and computer vision, I turn algorithmic training into choreography — an iterative dialogue between code, mechanics, and environment. I train my robotic systems to learn movement patterns and adapt their behavior. Training begins in simulated environments, primarily physics-based simulators, with occasional integration of motion capture or projection-based feedback, where machines rehearse gestures before performing them in physical space. This transition from virtual to embodied context is not a passage from artificial to real — it is a passage between two operative worlds, and movement gains complexity when friction, resistance, and material presence reorganize the field the robot must act within. Each sculpture tests how artificial behavior can feel expressive, even within strict mechanical and algorithmic limits.

My work asks what it means for a machine to move and to be seen moving. I am drawn to those moments when robotic motion feels alive yet remains unmistakably artificial — when apparently autonomous technology sharpens our awareness of what it means to move, to lose coherence, and to reorganize — to be a body sensing its own limits. I invoke mechanical function as an expressive act. This work joins a dialogue between art, engineering, and the philosophy of technology, demonstrating that movement can be both mechanical and poetic, and that gesture serves as a bridge between matter and meaning. In that tension, I think about life, perception, and creation as processes shared between humans and machines.

What I Like to Do

The questions, problems, and creative pursuits that drive my work.

I'm passionate about bridging the gap between simulation and reality — building embodied AI systems that generate expressive movement in digital spaces and translate that expressiveness to physical robots. My work repositions robotics as a medium for visual and performative art, emphasizing the interplay between computation, embodiment, and affect.

I care about exploring the aesthetic and political dimensions of technology. With a background in biology and media studies, I create multimedia pieces that challenge perceptions of the digital and natural worlds through dynamic interactions. I'm fascinated by how machine learning, generative models, and computer vision can expand our understanding of what it means to be embodied in an increasingly digital age.

What excites me most is the moment when code becomes experience — when a neural network trained in simulation produces movement that feels alive on a physical robot, when a generative model creates visual poetry that resonates emotionally, when an interactive 3D environment invites exploration of autonomous artificial life. I'm driven by questions like: How does it move? How does it feel? What happens when the machine becomes disobedient?

Beyond the technical, I'm invested in education and creative strategy — developing visual narratives, mentoring students, and building bridges between art and technology that make complex ideas accessible and compelling.
